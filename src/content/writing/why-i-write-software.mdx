There's a specific moment in every project that I keep coming back to. It's not the moment a feature ships, or when the tests finally go green. It's the moment when a problem that seemed impossibly tangled suddenly has a shape. When you can hold it in your head, turn it over, and see where the seams are.

That's why I write software.

## The thing about constraints

Software is almost entirely about constraints. Memory, time, bandwidth, backwards compatibility, the API you didn't design, the database schema from six years ago that everyone's afraid to touch. Most of the interesting work is finding a path through those constraints that doesn't feel like you've painted yourself into a corner.

Good constraints are clarifying. They tell you what matters. When you can't do everything, you have to decide what's actually important — and that decision is where design lives.

## Building versus shipping

There's a useful tension between *building* and *shipping*. Building is about getting something right. Shipping is about getting something out. The best engineers I've worked with are in constant negotiation between the two, never letting either one win completely.

The engineers who only build rarely ship anything anyone uses. The engineers who only ship accumulate debt that eventually makes shipping impossible.

The craft is in the negotiation.

## On writing about it

I write about software for the same reason I write software: to understand something better. Explaining a concept forces precision. It surfaces the gaps in your own mental model. It's a form of thinking out loud.

If any of this is useful to you, that's a bonus.
